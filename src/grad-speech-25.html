Giving back is one of the most important aspects and privileges of this scholarship. When I was interviewing for this program I spent a lot of time thinking about how I could give back. To me this meant mentoring students in the mentoring program and during orientation and development program events. But my conception of how to give back has changed since then.

When I think holistically about this program, and the study at this institution that it allows, we all develop three things: education, maturity and perspective. These traits form a strong basis with which you can change the lives of others.

Creating this change doesn’t necessarily mean advocating on government bodies (though some of you will do this), it can be more subtle. It can be the language choices you make in water-cooler conversations. Or it can be the example you set when you are given the privilege of teaching someone else.

The people in my life that I look up to the most, the role-models I aspire to emulate, are all emotionally intelligent, mature and thoughtful teachers who’s understanding and perspectives of the world I hope to gain. They are teachers who look-out, who’s conduct sets an example and who’s actions advocate for the opportunities of their students.

I want to encourage you to think about the teachers you look up to the most, to think about the ways in which you can emulate the example they set and to think about how you can be an advocate for those facing barriers to tertiary education.

However, implementing these things is no easy feat. The traits of these teachers are evidently the result of deep thinking and reflection; art crafted over many years.

For the 2022 and 2023 cohorts I encourage you all, when you get to teach, at work, Uni or home, to dedicate time to think deeply about how you can emulate your teachers and be an advocate. For the younger cohorts, I encourage you to participate in mentoring and to get as much out of this program that you can so you can learn the art of teaching and advocacy.

The consequence, for all cohorts, of doing these things is the ability to change the lives of others, as your teachers might have done for you and as this program has done for us all. As Hansen scholars—educated, mature and emotionally-intelligent young adults—you are all capable of becoming these kinds of teachers.

Doing this, to me, is the ultimate way that we give back to this program—a program which has given us so much.

The world is a better place when we give back. The world is a better place because of you all, Jane and Paul.

Thank you. Goodnight.
